About Kombucha

What is Kombucha?

The short answer is Kombucha is fermented tea! In a bit longer version, we can first look at regular teas. Tea leaves are always soaked in water, and while in the kettle they are sometimes cooled by pressing with a bit of pressure to prevent over-brewing. At the end of the boil, the water is cooled so it’s at least 70 degrees. Then the tea leaves are squeezed between the kettle and the wall of the refrigerator. The pressing creates the liquids that are eventually consumed during the brewing process. How does Kombucha Compare To Other teas? It is fermented ! Yes, it is fermented tea, meaning it is grown and brewed at a slightly elevated temperature and fermented for many months in the same way it is grown, which means Kombucha can have a different flavor and a different outcome compared to some other teas, as they might not ferment as well. But the result is a more potent tea. And once it ferments, the tea stays in the bottle for several weeks and continues to ferment in the refrigerator. At the end, the final product has a light and intense flavor. Similar to cider and beer, the process of making kombucha gives it many positive characteristics that improve its performance in the body.

Benefits:
The main benefit of kombucha is that it has the same weight-loss capacity as green tea but contains no tannin. As a result, it is a highly healthful beverage that is full of minerals and vitamins, but it has a slight acidic taste, so it won’t harm or irritate the stomach, throat, and stomach, which are the ones that we try to heal first during the diet. Kombucha has a lot of good components that can help our digestion and digestive system stay strong, nourished and healthy. In addition, it has tons of antioxidants and polyphenols (and therefore benefits the body from the inside out).

Does it have sugar?
Kombucha has zero sugar! Yes, the sugar is not in the kombucha. Instead, the sugar is added when you add sugar and flavorings to your drink. But you know what? The sugar and the juice from other fruits or vegetables aren’t necessary. They can be avoided if we follow the same good nutrition principles.

History

Kombucha most likely originated in the Bohai Sea district in China. The drink was consumed in Russia and from there entered the rest of Europe. Its consumption increased in the United States during the early 21st century. Having an alcohol content of less than 0.5%, kombucha is not a federally regulated beverage in the United States. Prior to 2015, some commercially available kombucha brands were found to contain alcohol content exceeding this threshold, sparking the development of new testing methods. With rising popularity in developed countries in the early 21st century, kombucha sales increased after it was marketed as an alternative to beer and other alcoholic drinks in restaurants and pubs. According to Grand View Research,[clarification needed] Kombucha had a global market size of US$1.64billion as of 2019 and this was expected to grow to US$2.4billion by 2027.
